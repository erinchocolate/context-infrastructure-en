# M8. Self-Systematization

## 1. Core Axiom

Self-systematization is building a hybrid organism: human + tools + logs + AI, making external artifacts a part of how you think and operate. This is not simple efficiency optimization, but a fundamental identity shift — evolving from a purely biological entity into a human-system hybrid, where files, logs, code, and AI become extensions of your cognition and externalizations of your memory.

## 2. Deep Reasoning

**Logs turn life into usable data**: Reflection is no longer based on feelings, but becomes something that can be queried, diffed, and learned from. When you continuously record time, decisions, and conversations, your life itself becomes a dataset that can be structured and analyzed by AI. The power of this transformation lies in letting you discover patterns in your life with a precision and scale that human intuition cannot reach — connections between work pressure and consumption behavior, the evolution of communication patterns, even hidden priority conflicts. But this also means you must consciously choose what to record and how to record it, otherwise you will be drowned by your own data.

**File-based memory is the nervous system of the hybrid**: If you want a persistent capability, write it in a form that an agent can reload. This includes design notes, decision logs, acceptance criteria, and even records of failures. Files are not just containers for information — they are the externalization and solidification of thought. When you write a vague idea into a document, you are forced to clarify details; when AI reads this document, it gains a reliable, traceable context. In multi-agent systems, this file-driven memory is especially critical — it becomes the communication medium between different agents, avoiding information loss in context windows.

**Tools compound non-linearly when combined**: A small number of carefully selected tools + stable external artifacts produce emergent capabilities. An Apple Watch combined with a Whisper model can give you superhuman hearing; a local agentic system combined with continuous life recording can make AI a cognitive partner rather than just a Q&A tool. This compounding comes from the synergy between tools — recording + transcription + structuring + retrieval + reflection, each layer amplifying the value of the previous one. But this also requires restraint in tool selection, because too many tools cause maintenance costs to explode, actually weakening the system's overall effectiveness.

**The risk is over-systematization**: Travel becomes travel + photos + blog + video, parenting moments get tied to equipment testing or course material. When you start viewing life through the lens of systems, it is easy to fall into a trap: life itself becomes input to the system, rather than the system serving life. You will find yourself thinking "this could be a blog post" or "this could be video material" while experiencing a beautiful moment. Doing this long-term, you lose the ability to simply "be in the moment" — those pure moments that don't need to be recorded, analyzed, or transformed into output. This is an invisible loss of life.

**Therefore, this hybrid needs a protected biological layer**: The system should capture feelings and moments, not just tasks and outputs. This means you need to consciously reserve some "non-recordable time" — casual chats with family, aimless walks, pure rest. These moments don't need to be transformed into data or output; their value lies in themselves. At the same time, you also need to regularly review your system and ask yourself: is this tool adding value to my life, or consuming my life?

## 3. Application Criteria

**When to Use**: When your projects and life context exceed what the brain can reliably carry (usually when you are simultaneously managing multiple long-term projects, or your work involves complex decision chains), or when you are building long-running agent workflows (SecondMind-style). More specifically, when you find yourself repeatedly giving the same background introduction to different AIs, or AI starts showing "forgetting" issues in large projects (frequent self-contradictory modifications appearing in projects over 5000 lines of code), that is the signal.

**How to Practice**: Make files the unit of memory — not recording for the sake of recording, but so your thoughts and decisions can be traced and reused. Design notes should record "why" not just "what"; decision logs should include context at the time and considered alternatives; acceptance criteria should be clear enough that AI (or your future self) can judge when a task is truly complete. Maintain a lightweight daily log — don't try to record everything, but record information that helps you discover patterns or make better decisions. Regularly run a "heartbeat" review: update the system while preserving space for non-productive moments. This review is not to optimize efficiency, but to ensure the system is still serving you rather than the other way around.

## 4. Tensions in Practice

The real challenge of self-systematization lies not in technology, but in psychological and ethical balance. When you start thinking about your life in the language of data and systems, you will face several deep questions:

**Blurring of identity**: Are you using AI, or is AI using you? When your life is continuously recorded, analyzed, and optimized, you gradually become a "humanoid computing node" — multithreading work, parenting, projects, and self-improvement. This highly efficient lifestyle can indeed produce more output, but it may also cause you to lose a clear answer to "why live." You may find that you are living for the system, rather than letting the system live for you.

**The curse of recording**: Once you start recording, it is hard to stop. Because stopping recording means losing data, and losing data means losing the opportunity for insight. This leads to compulsive recording behavior — even in moments unsuitable for recording (such as intimate time with your child), you unconsciously want to capture it. This is an invisible decline in life quality.

**System fragility**: A highly systematized life also has high fragility. If you depend on a certain tool or certain process, and it suddenly fails, the entire system may collapse. More dangerously, you may find you have already lost the ability to think and act independently without the system's help.

## 5. Signs of Maturity

A truly mature self-systematization practice should have the following characteristics:

- **Conscious choice**: You clearly know what you are recording, why you are recording, and how these records are used. You are not passively driven by the system, but actively designing and adjusting it.
- **Reserved space**: Your system has clearly defined "non-recordable zones" — parts of life you decide not to optimize, not to analyze, not to transform into output.
- **Regular reflection**: You not only use the system to analyze life, but also regularly reflect on the system itself. You ask yourself: is this system still serving me? Is it consuming my life rather than enhancing it?
- **Balance between human and system**: You can both leverage the power of the system to handle complex decisions and large-scale projects, and when necessary, set the system aside and return to a purely human state.
