# M5. Simplicity Is Cognitive Efficiency

## 1. Core Axiom

Choose the simplest system you will actually run every day, because every additional layer of process steals attention from the work itself. Cognitive resources are scarce, and system complexity has an exponential relationship with maintenance cost. A paper-and-pen checklist you actually use always beats a carefully designed tool that requires a startup ritual.

## 2. Deep Reasoning

### 2.1 Competition for Cognitive Resources

Working memory is the truly scarce resource. When your system forces you to remember too many things — state, shortcuts, structure, rules, exceptions — it directly competes with actual thinking. This is not a marginal cost problem; it is an exponential cognitive tax. Each additional layer of process requires you to maintain an extra mental model in your head. When these mental models start interacting, complexity grows explosively.

In 2013, I abandoned a fancy org-mode "time management" configuration precisely because managing it had become work itself. The time I spent adjusting shortcuts, designing project structures, and maintaining auto-generated schedules far exceeded the time the system saved me. Worse, every time I looked at that beautiful schedule, my brain was split between two things: "what should I do" and "what does my system say I should do." This split itself was a form of cognitive drain. After switching to paper and pen, the workflow became "write it down → go do it," with no intermediary in between. My brain could fully focus on the task itself, rather than being scattered by system maintenance.

### 2.2 Friction and Startup Energy

Low-friction tools compound, because they require no startup energy. This is a severely underestimated factor. A system that requires opening an app, logging in, navigating to the correct view, and then starting work consumes a portion of your willpower each time you use it. This consumption is cumulative. If you do this 10 times a day, that is 10x the startup cost. A paper-and-pen checklist that is always in front of you, available at any time, eliminates this cost.

This is why I explicitly prefer capabilities like automatic tracking and backup. RescueTime tracks my time without me doing anything; Dropbox doesn't require me to manually back up; Time Machine doesn't require me to remember backup dates. The value of these tools is not what they do, but that they let me not have to think about them. They work in the background, zero friction. By contrast, a system that requires me to manually enter data every day, even if more powerful in features, will gradually be abandoned due to friction.

### 2.3 Boundaries of Complexity and Resetting

Simple systems are easier to keep bounded. This is a key insight. When your system becomes too complex, the only solution is to rewrite it. And what is the cost of rewriting? Zero. You just take out a fresh sheet of paper and rewrite your to-do list. This reset process itself is a filter: you will only write down what is truly important, rather than carrying over all the historical baggage.

By contrast, a grand plan endlessly revised only accumulates process debt. Every time you add a new rule, a new category, a new view, you increase the system's complexity. And these additions are often irreversible — you can hardly delete a feature that is already in use, because someone might depend on it. The result is that the system becomes increasingly complex, increasingly difficult to maintain, and ultimately becomes a relic you no longer use.

### 2.4 The Power of Separation of Modes

Separation of modes is important. The next action should be frictionless and always visible; macro thinking deserves separate time blocks and a separate page. This principle comes from the core insight of GTD (Getting Things Done), but my practice shows its value far exceeds GTD itself.

What happens when you mix "next actions" with "macro thinking"? You fall into constant context switching. While executing a task, you suddenly think of a big strategic question, and your attention is pulled away. Or the reverse — while doing macro planning, you are interrupted by an urgent small task. The cost of this switching is huge. Research shows it takes 15-25 minutes to restore full cognitive capacity after switching from one task to another.

The solution is physical separation. My notebook has two parts: one is "today's next steps," always in front of me; the other is "macro thinking," which I only open at specific times (such as weekends). This separation not only reduces cognitive load, but also lets me switch more clearly between the two modes. When I am in execution mode, I know I do not need to think about the big picture; when I am in planning mode, I know I do not need to worry about today's details.

### 2.5 Simplicity in a Multi-Threaded Life

In a multi-threaded, fast-paced life, simplicity becomes even more critical. When you are simultaneously handling work, family, and multiple interest projects, your cognitive resources are already scattered. In this situation, your system must be extremely simple, otherwise it becomes yet another thread that needs to be managed.

The 2024 time-record analysis showed my life spans multiple domains: childcare, video editing, 3D printing, automation programming, household maintenance. Each domain has its own tasks and deadlines. If I tried to manage all of this with a complex system, I would be overwhelmed by the system itself. Instead, my strategy is to maintain an extremely simple "next steps" list, focusing each day on only the 3-5 most important items. Everything else is placed in a simple "to-do" list, but I do not try to prioritize or organize it. This "coarse-grained" management approach might seem insufficiently refined, but its benefit is that I am never troubled by system maintenance.

### 2.6 Relationship to Other Axioms

Simplicity complements M1 (Closed-Loop Calibration). Closed loops require fast feedback, and complex systems slow down feedback. A simple system lets you see results faster, and thus adjust faster. Simplicity also relates to M4 (Active Management): when your system is simple enough, you can invest more energy in active management rather than passively maintaining the system.

Simplicity also relates to X03 (Efficiency Is Determined by Bottlenecks). In a complex system, the bottleneck is often not the work itself, but the system's maintenance. By simplifying the system, you eliminate this bottleneck and let the actual work become the limiting factor.

## 3. Application Criteria

### When to Use

Personal efficiency systems, repetitive workflows, team processes, knowledge consolidation, or any time you find yourself spending more time on "organizing" than executing. Simplicity is especially essential in the following scenarios:

- **Personal task management**: If your to-do list system takes more than 30 seconds to add a new task, it is too complex.
- **Team processes**: When a process requires more than three steps to complete a common task, it is too complex. Team members will start bypassing it, causing confusion.
- **Knowledge management**: When your note-taking system requires you to spend time "organizing" notes rather than "writing" notes, it has failed.
- **AI collaboration**: When your instructions to AI require more than a page to explain, your system is probably too complex. Simple instructions often produce better results.

### How to Practice

1. **Continuously remove steps**: Start from your system, then ask yourself: is this step truly necessary? What happens if I remove it? Repeat this process until the workflow becomes "write it down → go do it."

2. **Maintain a single next-action list**: Don't have multiple lists, multiple priority systems, multiple views. One list, at most 10 items per day. If it exceeds 10 items, that means your priorities are the problem, not that you need a more complex system.

3. **Rewrite rather than over-edit when things get messy**: When your system becomes chaotic, don't try to fix it. Just rewrite it. Take out a fresh sheet of paper and start over. This process automatically filters out what is not important.

4. **Use occasional macro reviews to replace pushing macro thinking into daily execution**: Don't try to do strategic thinking every day during execution. Instead, schedule a fixed time (such as every weekend) for macro reviews. During this time, you can think about the big picture, adjust direction, and re-prioritize. But at other times, you only focus on execution.

5. **Choose zero-friction tools**: For features you need to use often, choose tools that require no startup ritual. Paper and pen, simple text files, auto-backed-up cloud storage — these are all zero-friction. Avoid tools that require logging in, navigating, and configuring.

6. **Regularly review your system**: Every month, ask yourself: is my system helping me, or dragging me down? If the latter, simplify it. Don't be attached to a system that isn't working.

### Common Pitfalls

- **Over-engineering**: Spending too much time designing the "perfect" system rather than actually using it. Remember, an imperfect system you actually use always beats a perfect system you don't.
- **Feature creep**: Continuously adding new features and rules until the system becomes unmanageable. Each time you add a new feature, ask yourself: is this truly worth the added complexity?
- **Multi-tool syndrome**: Using multiple tools to manage the same thing. This leads to scattered information and sync problems. Stick with one tool unless there is a very good reason.
- **Ignoring friction costs**: Underestimating the cost of startup rituals. A system that takes 2 minutes to start seems like nothing, but if you use it 10 times a day, that is 20 minutes wasted.
- **Treating the system as the goal**: Sometimes people start optimizing the system itself and forget that the system's purpose is to help them complete their work. If you find yourself spending more time optimizing the system than doing actual work, that is a signal the system is too complex.

## 4. Real-World Cases

### Case 1: From Org-Mode to Paper and Pen

In 2013, I used Emacs org-mode for time management. The system included:
- Multi-level project structure
- Auto-generated schedule
- Complex tags and priority system
- Hourly time tracking

The system looked powerful, but in reality:
- Adding a new task took 3-5 minutes (find the right project, set priority, add tags)
- Time spent maintaining the system exceeded the time the system saved me
- I was frequently troubled by the system's complexity rather than the tasks themselves

After switching to paper and pen:
- Adding a new task takes 10 seconds
- My brain can fully focus on the task itself
- When the system becomes messy, I just rewrite a fresh list

Result: increased productivity, reduced stress, improved focus.

### Case 2: Team Process Simplification

A team's code review process originally included:
1. Create PR
2. Add labels and milestones
3. Assign reviewers
4. Wait for automated checks
5. Conduct code review
6. Resolve comments
7. Get approval
8. Merge

This process had 8 steps, and each step had its own rules and exceptions. The result was that developers frequently skipped certain steps, causing confusion.

Simplified process:
1. Create PR
2. Wait for automated checks and code review
3. Merge

Only 3 steps. Labels, milestones, and assignments are all automated, or simply not needed. The result was that the process was followed more consistently and was faster.

### Case 3: Simplicity in Knowledge Management

A common trap is creating a complex note-taking system with multiple categories, tags, links, etc. Then you spend time "organizing" notes rather than "writing" notes.

A simpler approach:
- One folder, all notes inside
- Named by date (YYYY-MM-DD)
- Use full-text search to find notes
- Don't try to pre-organize

This system looks primitive, but its benefits are:
- Adding a note takes 5 seconds
- Finding a note takes 2 seconds
- No maintenance cost
- Over time, patterns naturally emerge from the notes

## 5. Deep Insights

The true value of simplicity lies not in saving time (though it does save time), but in protecting cognitive resources. In a world of information explosion, too many choices, and distractions everywhere, your attention is the most valuable resource. A simple system lets you concentrate this resource on what truly matters.

This is also why simplicity is especially relevant to a multi-threaded life. When you are simultaneously handling multiple projects, multiple roles, multiple interests, your cognitive resources are already scattered. In this situation, your system must be extremely simple, otherwise it becomes yet another thread that needs to be managed. Simplicity is not a luxury — it is a necessity.

Finally, simplicity is also a philosophical stance. It reflects an acceptance of "good enough" rather than a pursuit of "perfection." A good-enough system you actually use always beats a perfect system you don't. This principle applies to many areas of life, not just task management.
